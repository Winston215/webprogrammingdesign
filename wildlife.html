Indonesia: A Megadiverse Nation

17%

of World's Species

Indonesia is recognized as one of the planet's megadiverse countries, harboring approximately 17% of the Earth's total species. Its unique position, bridging the Indomalayan and Australasian realms, has cultivated an extraordinary array of flora and fauna, with many species found exclusively within its borders.

This remarkable biodiversity is spread across a vast archipelago of over 17,000 islands, encompassing diverse ecosystems from lush rainforests to vibrant marine environments.

Iconic Species

Orangutan

Found in Sumatra and Borneo, these great apes are critically endangered and known for their intelligence.

Sumatran Tiger

The smallest surviving tiger subspecies, found only on the island of Sumatra and critically endangered.

Javan Rhinoceros

One of the rarest large mammals on Earth, now found only in Ujung Kulon National Park.

Komodo Dragon

The world's largest lizard, native to a few Indonesian islands including Komodo and Rinca.

Birds of Paradise

Primarily found in Papua, these birds are famous for their elaborate plumage and courtship displays.

Babirusa

A pig-like mammal found in Sulawesi, known for its unique upward-curving tusks.

Indonesia is at the heart of the Coral Triangle, the area with the highest marine biodiversity on Earth. The country's waters are home to over 3,000 species of fish, 600 coral species, and numerous marine mammals, including whales, dolphins, and dugongs.

Raja Ampat: The Crown Jewel

Raja Ampat in West Papua is particularly renowned for its marine biodiversity, with scientists recording over 1,300 fish species and 600 coral species in this area alone. This underwater paradise is considered one of the most biodiverse marine regions on the planet.
